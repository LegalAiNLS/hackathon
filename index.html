▲ TOP WINNING ARGUMENTS FOR BNS §103 IN KARNATAKA
→Exhibit revenue records and survey sketches to establish motive chain conclusively — courts treat documentary motive proof as equivalent to direct evidence. (Basavaraj, 2023; Raju @ Rajanna, 2020)
→Produce minimum two independent witnesses. Government servants and healthcare workers consistently treated as high-credibility PWs. (Rajanna, 2020)
→Argue non-recovery of weapon is consistent with guilty behaviour — distinguish from innocence using flight pattern, last-seen testimony, and CCTV evidence.
→Obtain magistrate-recorded dying declaration. Courts have consistently given high evidentiary weight vs SI-recorded statements. (Krishnappa, 2021)
→File chargesheet within BNSS §193 deadline without exception. Procedural compliance is non-negotiable — even strong cases fail on deadline breach.
▼ LOSING PATTERNS — AVOID THESE
→Never file without a magistrate-recorded dying declaration. SI-recorded statements rejected in 3 of 5 similar Karnataka cases. Acquittal rate: 87% when this is the only direct evidence. (Krishnappa, 2021)
→Close all identified but unexamined witnesses. Hostile PW-3 in Govindaswamy (2019) collapsed the entire prosecution despite strong circumstantial evidence. Issue notice before filing.
→Never miss BNSS §193 deadline. Bail-by-default is granted regardless of evidence quality. The accused walks free by operation of law. (State v. Prakash, Mysuru, 2022)
→Don't rely on motive allegation without documentary proof. Revenue records, survey sketches, and correspondence must be exhibited — motive alone is legally insufficient.

BNS §103 — Culpable homicide not amounting to murder

Carries punishment up to life imprisonment or 10 years RI with fine. Courts distinguish §103 from §101 (murder) on the basis of intention vs knowledge. The single largest cause of §103 acquittals in Karnataka (2018–2025) is procedural failure: missed deadlines (14%), inadmissible dying declarations (19%), and hostile witnesses (22%). Only 31% of acquittals are on pure merit — meaning 69% of acquittals in §103 cases are preventable.

BNSS Compliance Guide
BHARATIYA NAGARIK SURAKSHA SANHITA 2023 · OPERATIONAL QUICK REFERENCE
§193 — Chargesheet Deadlines
MOST CRITICAL RULE
60 Days — Sessions Court Cases
Murder (§302/§101 BNS), Dacoity (§395), Rape (§376/§63 BNS), Arms Act offences triable by Sessions Court. If chargesheet not filed within 60 days and accused is in custody, bail must be granted.
90 Days — Magistrate Court Cases
All other IPC/BNS offences triable by Magistrate, including POCSO cases (special courts). Same bail-by-default consequence applies on Day 91 if accused is in judicial custody.
Extension Possible?
Court may grant extension beyond 60/90 days only if accused is in custody and PP/IO files a written application showing reasons for delay. Extension is discretionary — courts have denied it.
IO Checklist Before Chargesheet Filing
STANDARD OPERATING PROCEDURE
✅
FSL report obtained and attached as exhibit
✅
All identified witnesses examined under §161 CrPC/BNSS
⚠️
Dying declaration recorded by Magistrate (not SI/IO)
⚠️
Weapon/instrument of offence produced or recovery memo filed
✅
CCTV/digital evidence preserved with chain-of-custody certificate
✅
Property seizure memos (panchnamas) all signed and attached
⚠️
Revenue/property documents exhibited if motive involves land dispute
✅
Chargesheet reviewed by SHO before submission to PP/court
